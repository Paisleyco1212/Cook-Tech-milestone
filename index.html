Soon they found that the keyboard not only worked, but it was highly useful and even more so covenant than what they had been using before hand. Just think. A-0 1952

Grace Hopper, an English mathematician and programmer. In 1952 she did something absolutely amazing, something that had changed the world of computers forever. She completed the A-0, which stands for Arithmetic Language version 0. The A-0 System was a set of instructions that could translate symbolic mathematical code into machine language. But even with something amazing like this, she was first met with disbelief and non acceptance. People could not believe that the computer could program itself, now computers back them could only do athermitic. It took two years of her trying to get others to even look at it, but then it became accepted. Grace Hopper’s work paved the way to more advanced programming languages. Ones like Python, or even HTML like how this website was Coded.

The LaserDisc 1978

Listing to your fave tunes in the car didn't used to be hooking up your phone to the car via an ox cord, and you couldn't watch you favorite movie on netflix. No instead we had CDs and DVDs, and even before then there were LaserDisc. The LaserDisc was one of the early home video formats, alongside VHS. LaserDisc had an edge over VHS though as it provides better quality video and audio. The LaserDisc was not very popular in North America, Europe, or Australia. But in Japan and other Southeast Asin regions, they loved the LaserDisc. LaserDiscs were used as the foundation for CDs, DVDs, and blu-rays.

The First Computer Program Ran on a Computer 1956

People call things they love, Baby, a term of endearment for things we love. In 1948 the Manchester “Baby”, or the Small-Scale Experimental Machine (SSEM), was built to test new memory technology. The first program was seventeen instructions ran on June 21st, 1948. This was a big deal as this was the first computer program ever successfully ran. Freddie Williams, a man who worked on the computer, is quoted to say this on the day of the successful run, “This was in June 1948, and nothing was ever the same again."
